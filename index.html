Virtual Reality in Education

Introduction

From an early age, we are often told that learning goes beyond the classroom, and this idea remains true. Education is not limited to the content taught in lectures, but is also shaped by how knowledge is applied, practiced, and reflected on outside the traditional classroom setting. While the founders of education have held us to this standard, it is time we move beyond the classroom and understand how Virtual Reality (VR) can transform learning as we know it.

VR is defined by the University of Baltimore as “a computer-generated, immersive, and interactive experience that simulates a three-dimensional environment, often with the aid of specialized hardware such as headsets and controllers” (UMB Faculty Center for Teaching and Learning, n.d.). In short, VR is an emerging educational technology that allows students to engage with immersive simulations and lectures.

Incorporating VR can make a significant impact in the education industry, especially alongside other learning styles, such as visual learners who might benefit from moving away from traditional classroom settings that rely heavily on lectures and textbooks. Additionally, VR provides the excitement that many students crave in academia and has the potential to increase student engagement.

The push for VR is not just about changing the teaching format in schools, but about renewing students’ love for learning. VR enables students to reinforce textbook learning by offering deeper interaction with concepts. Learning should not be limited to the classroom; VR gives students the chance to explore beyond perceived obstacles. It is more than a teaching trend—it is already being used in nursing programs to ensure that a graduating nurse’s first physical patient is not their first real patient. Alongside this, VR allows students to visit museums, landmarks, and environments that would otherwise be inaccessible.

VR for Medical and Nursing Training

Osso VR is a virtual reality surgical training platform used by medical schools and hospitals such as Harvard Medical School, UCSF, and the Mayo Clinic. It allows students and doctors to practice surgical procedures in a realistic 3D virtual environment using VR headsets and hand controls.

Context and How It Is Used

VR is used to provide medical and nursing students with hands-on, risk-free experiences. Students enter a virtual operating room, follow step-by-step instructions, perform procedures, and receive immediate feedback. These simulations can be repeated multiple times, helping learners build confidence before treating real patients.

Social Challenges

  • Cost and Access: VR equipment is expensive, creating disparities between well-funded and underfunded medical programs.
  • Skill Transfer: Virtual training cannot fully replace real-world patient interaction or human mentorship.
  • Technical Limitations: Motion sickness, hardware maintenance, and IT support needs may limit usability.
  • Data Concerns: Student performance data may raise privacy and evaluation issues.

Why This Matters

VR training improves patient safety, increases student confidence, and helps standardize medical education. However, concerns surrounding equity, realism, and privacy must be addressed to ensure ethical adoption.

VR Field Trips in Education

Real-World Example: Google Arts & Culture VR

Google Expeditions was one of the most widely used VR classroom tools before becoming part of Google Arts & Culture. Today, the platform provides VR-based virtual field trips used in K–12 classrooms worldwide, allowing students to explore historical sites, museums, and cultural landmarks through immersive environments.

Context and Purpose

Google Arts & Culture VR brings real-world locations into classrooms for subjects such as history, geography, and global studies. Students can explore ancient ruins, walk along the Great Wall of China, and experience historical civilizations. Teachers guide these activities by selecting destinations and facilitating discussion.

The purpose of VR field trips is to increase engagement, make abstract concepts more tangible, and support learners who benefit from visual and experiential learning.

Ethical and Social Concerns

Access to VR technology is unequal, as many schools lack funding or sufficient internet access. This creates educational gaps between districts. There are also concerns regarding cultural accuracy and representation. Without careful design, VR experiences may simplify or misrepresent historical events. Additionally, teachers may lack training to integrate VR effectively, and student data collection raises privacy concerns.

Ethical Analysis

Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism evaluates actions based on whether they benefit the greatest number of people. From this perspective, VR can increase student engagement across cognitive, behavioral, and emotional dimensions. Interactive 3D environments can improve understanding of complex topics, encourage participation, and boost motivation.

However, improper implementation, limited teacher training, and technical issues can reduce effectiveness and waste resources. From a utilitarian viewpoint, VR is ethical only when its benefits outweigh its costs.

Justice and Fairness

The justice lens focuses on equal access and fair distribution of resources. VR can support students with diverse learning needs and offer adaptive learning environments. However, disparities in access risk widening existing educational inequalities.

Ethically, VR implementation is justified only when schools receive equitable funding, training, and access to resources.

Recommendations

Schools and Educators

  • Provide teacher training and technical support.
  • Use VR as a supplement rather than a replacement for traditional instruction.
  • Design inclusive experiences accessible to all learners.

Policymakers

  • Support under-resourced schools through equitable funding.
  • Develop standards for safe and ethical VR use in classrooms.
  • Monitor learning outcomes to ensure fairness.

Technology Companies

  • Create affordable and user-friendly VR solutions.
  • Collaborate with educators to develop classroom-ready content.
  • Provide training materials to support adoption.
